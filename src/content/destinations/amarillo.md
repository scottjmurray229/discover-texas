Amarillo is the city you drive through on the way to somewhere else, and that's entirely your loss. I drove into town from Oklahoma on I-40 at dusk, the Panhandle sky doing what Panhandle skies do — spreading a sunset across 180 degrees of horizon in bands of orange and purple that seem to actively resent any landscape that might limit their scale. I stopped at Cadillac Ranch first, because it was right there on the frontage road: ten Cadillacs buried nose-first in a wheat field, covered in spray paint applied in layers going back to 1974. The current layer was neon green and pink. Someone had written something in Spanish on the bumper of the last car. The sun set behind me and the cars glowed. I stood there for 20 minutes feeling like I was inside a piece of American mythology.

Cadillac Ranch is free, open 24 hours, and invites you to spray paint it. The highway-side art piece — created by the artist collective Ant Farm on the instruction of eccentric Amarillo millionaire Stanley Marsh 3 — has been continuously repainted since 1974, and bringing a spray can is as traditional as bringing a camera. The hardware stores nearby sell single cans for $2. I wrote my name on the rear window of a 1963 Sedan de Ville and felt like I had signed something important.

Palo Duro Canyon is 25 minutes southeast of Amarillo, and it's the real reason to come here. The Grand Canyon of Texas drops 800 feet below the Panhandle plains and stretches 120 miles in a landscape that is completely invisible until you're at the rim. See the Palo Duro Canyon destination page for the full details — it deserves its own entry.

The Big Texan Steak Ranch on I-40 is part tourist attraction, part genuine Texas institution, and completely itself. The 72-ounce steak challenge (eat the steak plus accompaniments in one hour and it's free) has been running since 1960. The steak itself — a 4.5-pound sirloin — is good regardless of whether you're attempting the challenge, and the restaurant's garish neon and taxidermy décor is the most concentrated expression of Amarillo's self-image in a single building.

## Why Amarillo is quintessentially Texas

Amarillo is the capital of the Texas Panhandle, a rectangular chunk of land added to Texas when the Kansas border was drawn in 1850. The Panhandle produces more beef cattle than almost any comparable region in America — the feedlots surrounding Amarillo are visible from I-40 and produce an aroma that is unmistakable. The city also sits at the center of the largest single aquifer in North America (the Ogallala Aquifer), which has been draining faster than it refills for decades in one of the defining water stories of the American West.

Route 66 is the other Amarillo identity. The historic highway ran through Amarillo from 1926 until it was decommissioned in 1985, and the 6th Street historic district preserves the best stretch of vintage Route 66 business culture in Texas — neon signs, diners, ice cream shops, and bars that have been serving travelers since the Dust Bowl era. It's genuinely atmospheric and not yet fully gentrified.

The Panhandle's weather is in a category of its own. This is tornado country — the southern end of Tornado Alley — and the storms that build here in spring are some of the most dramatic weather events in the continental US. The flat landscape with no obstructions means you can watch a supercell thunderstorm develop and track toward you for two hours before it arrives. Storm chasing tourism is a legitimate industry in Amarillo in May and June.

## What should you do in Amarillo?

**Cadillac Ranch** — Free, 24 hours, I-40 frontage road west of town. Bring spray paint. Stay for sunset. This is one of the most purely American art experiences in the country.

**Palo Duro Canyon State Park** — 25 minutes southeast. See the dedicated destination page for full hiking and activity details. This is the primary reason to come to Amarillo.

**Big Texan Steak Ranch** — Challenge or no challenge, eat here. The 72-oz steak is served at a raised table while the dining room watches. The regular menu has excellent steaks. The taxidermy and neon are irreplaceable.

**Route 66 Historic District (6th Street)** — Walk the preserved stretch of Route 66 with vintage neon signs, independent restaurants, and bars that have been operating since the highway was commissioned. Best in the evening when the neon is lit.

**Panhandle-Plains Historical Museum** — In Canyon, 15 minutes south. The largest history museum in Texas with an outstanding collection covering Panhandle Plains Native cultures, the cattle industry, and West Texas oil. Free with Canyon (town) admission.

**Wonderland Amusement Park** — The regional amusement park in Thompson Park has been operating since 1951. Good for families.

**Amarillo Botanical Garden** — A genuine surprise — 5.5 acres of well-maintained gardens in a city not known for gardens. The tropical conservatory is excellent in winter.

**Storm Chasing Tours** — Multiple operators offer spring storm chasing tours into the Panhandle. May–June season produces some of the most dramatic supercell thunderstorms in North America.

## Where should you eat in Amarillo?

- **Big Texan Steak Ranch** — 72-oz challenge optional. The regular cuts are excellent — grain-fed Panhandle beef. The cheesy atmosphere is part of the meal. $$
- **Tyler's Barbeque** — Consistently cited as the best BBQ in Amarillo. Brisket, ribs, and sausage without pretension. $
- **Atlanta's Tex-Mex Grill** — Outstanding enchiladas and breakfast tacos from a family-owned operation that has built a loyal local following. $
- **Crush Wine Bar** — The best wine list in Amarillo with excellent small plates. The most sophisticated dining option in the city. $$$
- **Coyote Bluff Café** — The Green Chile Cheeseburger here is frequently cited as one of the best burgers in Texas. Counter service, cash only. $
- **Golden Light Cantina** — Route 66 institution since 1946. Cold Lone Star, live country music on weekends, and Tex-Mex that has barely changed since Truman was president. $
- **Hoffbrau Steaks** — Traditional Texas steakhouse with mesquite-grilled steaks at prices that Austin and Dallas can't match. $$
- **Ohms Homestyle Cooking** — Breakfast and lunch focused on Panhandle comfort food. The chicken fried steak is locally revered. $

## Where should you stay in Amarillo?

**Budget ($50–$80/night):** Amarillo has excellent chain hotel value — the Motel 6 and Super 8 along I-40 are reliable and cheap. The Holiday Inn Express downtown is a step up at still-affordable rates.

**Mid-range ($80–$150/night):** The Ambassador Hotel downtown is a beautifully restored 1930 Art Deco property with reasonable rates and the most character in the city. The Big Texan Steak Ranch Motel (adjacent to the restaurant) is the full Amarillo experience in one building.

**Luxury ($150–$250/night):** The Hyatt Place Amarillo and the Courtyard Marriott downtown are the best full-service options. Rates remain very affordable compared to other Texas cities.

## When is the best time to visit Amarillo?

May through June and September through October are the ideal seasons for Palo Duro Canyon hiking and pleasant outdoor temperatures. April and May bring storm season — if you're interested in severe weather, this is a fascinating time to be in the Panhandle (with appropriate caution and awareness). Summer is hot (95–100°F) but dry. Winter brings Panhandle winds and occasional ice storms that make driving hazardous — the flat terrain offers zero wind protection.

Amarillo is not a destination most people plan around, and that's the point — it's a place that rewards the unplanned stop, the curiosity about what's in the wheat field off the frontage road, the willingness to eat a 72-ounce steak in front of strangers. Come for Palo Duro Canyon, stop at Cadillac Ranch, eat at Tyler's BBQ, and keep the Big Texan in reserve for when the canyon has given you an appropriate appetite.
