Glen Rose has something genuinely remarkable: in the limestone bed of the Paluxy River, preserved under water and exposed at low flow, are the actual footprints of dinosaurs from 113 million years ago. Not replicas. Not casts. The original tracks pressed into mud that lithified into limestone while the continents were still rearranging themselves. You can stand in them. You can see the texture of the toes. You can follow the trail across the river bed and imagine the sauropod — 60 feet long, 30 tons — walking through what was then a shallow tidal flat on the shore of an inland sea.

Dinosaur Valley State Park covers the main tracksites on the Paluxy River, and for $7 per person it's one of the great natural history bargains in Texas. The sauropod tracks (Sauroposeidon) and theropod tracks (Acrocanthosaurus — the apex predator of North America 113 million years ago) tell a story of predator and prey at a scale and antiquity that the mind struggles to accommodate. The tracks are most visible when river levels are low — late summer through early winter — and the experience of walking in the same river crossing as a 60-ton dinosaur is one that changes your sense of time in a useful way.

Fossil Rim Wildlife Center 3 miles west is the other anchor — a 1,800-acre conservation breeding facility for endangered species. The drive-through experience puts you among giraffes, zebras, cheetahs, and white rhinos with no fence between your car window and the animals. The giraffes will push their heads through your window for the animal feed you can buy at the gate. The rhinos lumber past with improbable bulk. The cheetahs are in a separate viewing area (for obvious reasons), but the African ungulate experience is genuinely immersive.

Glen Rose's small downtown has the antique shops, bakeries, and courthouse square character of a North Texas county seat that has been here since the 1870s, with the overlay of a tourism economy built on prehistoric tracks and modern conservation science. The combination is eccentric and specifically Texas.

## Why Glen Rose is quintessentially Texas

Glen Rose represents Texas's extraordinary geological and paleontological heritage — the state was seafloor, river delta, and tropical lowland at various points in deep time, and the fossil record is correspondingly rich. The Paluxy River dinosaur tracks are the most accessible and most spectacular example of this heritage: an educational experience that is also physically thrilling in a way that museum displays can't replicate.

The Creation Evidence Museum, also in Glen Rose, takes the position that the fossil record is consistent with a young earth interpretation. The coexistence of Dinosaur Valley State Park (mainstream paleontology) and the Creation Museum (creationist interpretation) in the same small Texas town tells you something about the breadth of Texas's relationship with science and religion — both coexist without irony or apparent tension, and both have visitors who come with conviction.

Fossil Rim's conservation mission represents a serious institutional response to the global extinction crisis. The facility has successfully bred multiple endangered species including the scimitar-horned oryx (declared extinct in the wild), Arabian oryx, Grevy's zebra, and multiple Mexican wolf subspecies. The drive-through visitor experience funds the conservation breeding programs. This is not a zoo — it's a working conservation facility that allows public access as a revenue source for the mission.

## What should you do in Glen Rose?

**Dinosaur Valley State Park** — $7/person. The main tracksites are in the Paluxy River bed — walk down to the water and you're in the tracks. The Blue Hole swimming area at the park is excellent. Allow 3–4 hours.

**Fossil Rim Wildlife Center** — $25/person drive-through. Buy animal feed at the gate. Giraffes, zebras, rhinos, and 50+ other species. The 9-mile drive takes 1.5–2 hours. The Behind the Scenes tours ($75–$150) add an educational layer.

**Paluxy River Swimming** — The state park's swimming holes on the Paluxy River are excellent spring through early summer. The Blue Hole at the end of the park road is the main swimming area.

**Glen Rose Downtown** — The historic courthouse square, antique shops, bakeries, and the Somervell County Courthouse (1893). Small but authentic North Texas county seat character.

**Somervell County Expo Center** — Check the schedule for local events — the county fair, livestock shows, and rodeo events reflect the agricultural community around Glen Rose.

**Squaw Creek Reservoir** — 10 miles southeast. Fishing for bass and catfish. The reservoir is less visited than the downtown attractions.

**Granbury Day Trip** — 30 minutes north. The preserved courthouse square on Lake Granbury and the Granbury Opera House pair well with Glen Rose for a weekend North Texas trip.

## Where should you eat in Glen Rose?

- **Rough Creek Lodge** — The luxury ranch lodge 8 miles north has a restaurant open to the public (with reservation) serving excellent Texas ranch cuisine. The best dinner option in the area. $$$
- **Fossil Rim Lodge Restaurant** — The lodge restaurant serves meals to overnight guests and day visitors (with reservation). Texas ranch cooking with the Fossil Rim setting. $$$
- **Stomping Grounds Coffee** — Best coffee and light breakfast in downtown Glen Rose. The morning gathering spot before heading to the park. $
- **Hammond's BBQ** — Local BBQ with brisket and ribs. Casual and affordable. The standard lunch stop for Dinosaur Valley visitors. $

## Where should you stay in Glen Rose?

**Camping at Dinosaur Valley ($20–$25/night):** Camp inside the state park within walking distance of the dinosaur tracks. The campsites along the Paluxy River are the most atmospheric in North Texas. Book via recreation.gov.

**Fossil Rim Safari Lodging ($200–$600+/night):** The Foothills Safari Camp at Fossil Rim offers glamping tents on platforms in the wildlife center — falling asleep to animal sounds with giraffes visible from your tent. An extraordinary experience at premium prices.

**Budget/Mid-range ($70–$140/night):** Chain hotels and independent motels in Glen Rose and nearby Granbury (30 miles north). Rough Creek Lodge 8 miles north is a luxury ranch resort at premium rates.

## When is the best time to visit Glen Rose?

March through May offers spring wildflowers, comfortable temperatures (65–85°F), and the state park's natural beauty at its best. The dinosaur tracks are most visible at lower water levels — late summer through early winter is typically best for track viewing, so September–November combines fall weather with optimal track visibility. Summer is hot but the Paluxy River swimming makes it manageable. Winter is mild and the park is uncrowded.

Glen Rose is the North Texas destination that families remember. Walking in actual dinosaur tracks, feeding giraffes through a car window, swimming in a river where prehistoric animals walked — none of this can be replicated anywhere else in Texas. Combine with Granbury 30 miles north for a weekend trip that covers the state park, the safari center, and one of North Texas's best historic courthouse squares.
