El Paso is the Texas that the rest of Texas forgets about, which is partly a function of geography — the city sits 745 miles from Houston and is technically closer to Los Angeles than to Dallas — and partly a function of identity. El Paso doesn't feel like Texas. It feels like the border, which is its own thing: a binational city of 2.5 million people (El Paso plus Ciudad Juarez) where English and Spanish switch mid-sentence, where the Franklin Mountains rise directly from the center of the city to 7,000 feet, and where the Mexican food is closer to Chihuahua than to San Antonio.

That Mexican food is what I came for, and it was outstanding. El Paso's cuisine is shaped by the convergence of Texas, New Mexico, and Chihuahua, Mexico, and the result is something distinct from any of them individually. The green chile that dominates New Mexico cooking flows down from the Hatch Valley 90 miles north and smothers everything here — burritos, enchiladas, chile rellenos — in a way that is completely different from the red-sauce Tex-Mex of San Antonio or the puffy-taco tradition of the same city. I ate at L&J Café on Oregon Street, a family restaurant that opened in 1927 and has been making the same green chile chicken enchiladas ever since, and I thought about how inadequately "Mexican food" describes the regional variations that stretch from this table to the Gulf Coast.

The Franklin Mountains are the visual centerpiece of El Paso — a range that rises directly from the city's center to 7,192 feet at North Franklin Peak, cutting the city into two valleys and providing a 24,000-acre urban wilderness park that is the largest of its kind in the United States. I took the Wyler Aerial Tramway to the 5,632-foot level on a clear October morning and looked out at New Mexico, Chihuahua, and three distinct landscapes simultaneously: the Chihuahuan Desert running south into Mexico, the Rio Grande valley curving east through El Paso and Juarez, and the Tularosa Basin of New Mexico spreading north toward Albuquerque.

Ciudad Juarez is a 20-minute walk from downtown El Paso through the international bridge. The narrative about Juarez being dangerous — while historically accurate for a specific period — is not the current reality for most visitors. I walked across, ate a torta at a market near the city center, bought pottery, and walked back. The experience of being in Mexico within 20 minutes of a Texas city is unique to El Paso and worth doing.

## Why El Paso is quintessentially Texas

El Paso represents the extreme of Texas's geographic scale — so far west that it's in a different time zone, closer to California than to East Texas, and more culturally aligned with New Mexico and Chihuahua than with Houston or Dallas. The city was a Spanish colonial outpost, then a Mexican border city, then an American border city after the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo in 1848, and the layers of those identities are all present in the architecture, the food, and the street names.

The border culture here is not a tourist attraction — it's how people live. El Paso families have relatives in Juarez, shop at markets on both sides, and navigate two national healthcare systems. The international bridges are commuter infrastructure, not tourist experiences (though they work fine for visitors too). The city's bilingualism is functional, not performative. This is what a genuinely binational city looks like.

El Paso is also one of the most affordable major cities in Texas — hotel rates, restaurant prices, and the general cost of living are significantly lower than Austin, Dallas, or Houston. This makes it an excellent value destination with a level of food quality and cultural depth that its price point doesn't advertise.

## What should you do in El Paso?

**Franklin Mountains State Park** — The largest urban wilderness park in the US, rising from the center of the city. The Wyler Aerial Tramway takes you to 5,632 feet with views of three states. The North Franklin Peak Trail (7.4 miles round-trip) reaches the highest point. $7 park entry plus $9 tramway.

**Ciudad Juarez Day Trip** — Walk across the El Paso Street Bridge (bring US passport). The Mercado Juarez, the historic mission churches, and the restaurants in the Zona Centro offer an authentic Mexican border city experience. The best day-trip option on the US-Mexico border in Texas.

**El Paso Museum of Art** — Downtown museum with a permanent collection that includes exceptional Spanish Colonial art from the missions and pre-Columbian objects. Free admission. The facility is excellent for its size.

**Tigua Indian Cultural Center** — The Ysleta del Sur Pueblo is the oldest surviving community in Texas, established by Tigua refugees from the Pueblo Revolt in 1680. The cultural center, museum, and casino are all worth visiting.

**Hueco Tanks State Park** — 30 miles northeast. Ancient rock art (pictographs) by multiple indigenous cultures over 4,000 years, plus some of the best bouldering in the United States. Guided tours required for pictograph sites. $7 entry.

**Mission Trail** — Three Spanish colonial missions (Ysleta, Socorro, and San Elizario) east of downtown form one of the oldest mission trails in the US, predating the famous California missions by over a century.

**UTEP Campus** — The University of Texas at El Paso's campus is architecturally unique — inspired by the Bhutanese dzong style at the request of a dean's wife who was inspired by a National Geographic article. The campus buildings look like Himalayan monasteries. Free to walk.

**Scenic Drive Overlook** — A short drive up the Franklin Mountain foothills to a parking area with panoramic views of the entire El Paso-Juarez metropolitan area. Best at sunset when the city lights spread across the valley floor.

## Where should you eat in El Paso?

- **L&J Café** — El Paso institution since 1927 on Oregon Street. Green chile chicken enchiladas that haven't changed in 90 years. Cash only. Family-operated. $
- **H&H Car Wash and Coffee Shop** — Breakfast in a car wash since 1958. The breakfast burritos with green chile are what El Paso mornings taste like. Counter seating only. $
- **Cattleman's Steakhouse at Indian Cliffs Ranch** — 35 miles east. Cowboy steaks cooked over mesquite wood at an actual working ranch. The most atmospheric steakhouse in Texas. $$
- **Leo's Mexican Food** — Multiple locations, family-owned since 1945. The best red enchiladas in El Paso, served with charro beans and Mexican rice. $
- **Chico's Tacos** — El Paso's unique fast food — rolled corn tortillas in a tomato broth soup with shredded beef and melted cheese. It sounds strange and it is extraordinary. Local institution. $
- **Elemi** — Modern Mexican fine dining in downtown El Paso. The most sophisticated Mexican cooking in the city. $$$
- **Craft Beer and Spirits in the Arts District** — The Union draft house, Deadbeach Brewery, and Whiskey Dick's populate the downtown arts district. $$
- **Juarez market food** — Cross the bridge for carnitas, tamales, and gorditas at the Mercado Juarez food stalls. Cheapest and freshest border food available. $

## Where should you stay in El Paso?

**Budget ($50–$90/night):** El Paso is one of the most affordable hotel markets in Texas. Chain hotels downtown and near UTEP offer clean rooms at excellent rates. The airport corridor has extended-stay options at very low prices.

**Mid-range ($90–$160/night):** The Courtyard El Paso Downtown/Convention Center is centrally located near the arts district. The Holiday Inn Express at the airport is reliable for early flights.

**Luxury ($160–$280/night):** The Camino Real Hotel (1912) is El Paso's historic landmark accommodation — the Tiffany Bar with its original stained glass dome is one of the most beautiful bar rooms in Texas. Recently renovated. The Marriott El Paso downtown has all modern amenities at rates well below comparable Dallas or Austin properties.

## When is the best time to visit El Paso?

El Paso has 300+ sunny days per year and a high desert climate (3,762 feet elevation) that is fundamentally different from the rest of Texas. March through May and October through November are ideal — temperatures 70–85°F, low humidity, and perfect hiking weather in the Franklin Mountains. Summer (June–August) is hot (95–105°F) but drier than anywhere else in Texas, making it more tolerable. Winter (December–February) is mild by day (50–65°F) and cold at night, with occasional snow on the Franklin Mountains that creates a dramatic visual contrast with the desert below.

El Paso is the Texas destination most consistently underrated by people who haven't been. Its safety reputation is the inverse of reality, its food is distinctive and excellent, its mountain park is extraordinary, and its border culture is genuinely unlike anything else in the state. Come here, eat at L&J Café, walk to Juarez, and hike the Franklins. You'll wonder why it took you so long.
